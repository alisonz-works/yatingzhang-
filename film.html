Documentary Film

The Ground We Learn to Call Home

A cross-cultural documentary about how people build a sense of “home” when the world around them cannot be changed — told through personal migration, lived spaces, and the physical language of place.

Why This Film

I began this documentary after realizing that, as an international student, I was constantly adapting to environments that did not adapt to me. Over time, “home” stopped feeling like a fixed place and started feeling like a fragile process — something built through repetition, memory, and survival.

The central question of this film is simple but difficult: If we cannot leave or change a place, how do people form a sense of home inside it?

Inspiration what influenced my approach

This project was inspired by stories of displacement, cross-cultural identity, and the way landscapes shape human behavior. I am especially interested in how the physical environment (distance, terrain, weather, and geological features) quietly influences what people believe, fear, and protect.

  • Personal experience: moving between cultures and households, rebuilding daily life from scratch
  • Documentary influence: observational storytelling that treats small details as evidence
  • Scientific influence: thinking in systems — how constraints shape outcomes

Concept & Structure from lived spaces to a bigger idea

The film begins in the United States with three homes I lived in — spaces that represent different stages of adaptation. As the story progresses, the film shifts toward a larger concept: environments that cannot be escaped, and the ways people construct “home” within those constraints.

  • Part I (U.S.): three households, daily details, and the emotional mechanics of adaptation
  • Part II (China): returning to familiar landscapes, re-examining what “home” means after change
  • Throughline: place as a silent force — shaping memory, identity, and belonging
